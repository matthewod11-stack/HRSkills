## Template 1: Startup Recognition Program (<50 employees)

**Company profile:**
- 30-50 employees
- Budget-constrained
- Scrappy, informal culture
- High growth (headcount doubling annually)

---

### Goals

1. Build recognition culture from day one (before bad habits form)
2. Make recognition frequent and peer-driven
3. Keep it simple (minimal admin overhead)
4. Low cost (maximize impact per dollar)

---

### Program Design

#### Component 1: Peer Shout-Outs (Free)

**Platform:** Slack channel (#shoutouts or #kudos)

**How it works:**
- Anyone can post a shout-out to recognize a colleague
- Post format:

```
üôå Shout-out to @Sarah for [specific thing they did]

Example:
üôå Shout-out to @Sarah for staying late to help me debug the production issue. You saved our demo. Thank you!
```

**Why it works:**
- Zero cost
- Low friction (just post in Slack)
- Public (builds positive culture)
- Frequent (can happen multiple times per day)

**Launch checklist:**
- [ ] Create #shoutouts Slack channel
- [ ] CEO posts first shout-out (models behavior)
- [ ] Add to new hire onboarding ("We use #shoutouts to recognize each other")
- [ ] Encourage managers to post at least 1 shout-out per week

---

#### Component 2: Manager Spot Bonuses

**Budget:** $500 per manager per quarter

**How it works:**
- Managers can give $50-$200 spot bonuses for great work
- No approval needed (trust managers)
- Announce publicly (team meeting or #shoutouts)

**Example:**
- Tom (Engineering Manager) gives Sarah $100 for fixing critical bug
- Posts in #shoutouts:

```
üí∞ Spot bonus: $100 to @Sarah for fixing the auth bug that was blocking our launch. You dropped everything to help. Thank you!
```

**Budget calculation:**
```
5 managers √ó $500/quarter = $2,500/quarter = $10K/year
```

---

#### Component 3: Service Anniversary Gifts

**Milestones:** 1 year, 3 years, 5 years

**Rewards:**

| Milestone | Gift | Cost |
|-----------|------|------|
| **1 year** | Company swag bundle (backpack, hoodie, water bottle) + handwritten note from CEO | $100 |
| **3 years** | $300 gift card + public shout-out at all-hands | $300 |
| **5 years** | $500 experience gift + 1 extra PTO week | $500 |

**Annual cost estimate:**
```
Assume 30% annual turnover (startup)
Year 1: 10 employees hit 1-year mark = $1K
Year 2: 6 employees hit 3-year mark = $1.8K
Year 3: 3 employees hit 5-year mark = $1.5K

Total: ~$4K-$5K/year (grows as company matures)
```

---

### Total Budget

| Component | Annual Cost |
|-----------|-------------|
| Peer shout-outs (Slack) | $0 |
| Manager spot bonuses | $10K |
| Service anniversaries | $4K-$5K |
| **Total** | **$14K-$15K/year** |

**Per employee:** $300-$500/year (very affordable)

---

### Implementation Plan

**Week 1-2: Design**
- [ ] Define program (peer shout-outs + spot bonuses + anniversaries)
- [ ] Get CEO buy-in
- [ ] Allocate budget ($15K/year)

**Week 3: Manager Training**
- [ ] Train managers on recognition (30-min session)
- [ ] Explain spot bonus budget ($500/quarter)
- [ ] Show examples of great recognition

**Week 4: Launch**
- [ ] Create #shoutouts Slack channel
- [ ] CEO sends launch email explaining program
- [ ] CEO posts first shout-out (models behavior)
- [ ] Managers start using spot bonus budget

**Month 2-3: Adoption**
- [ ] Track # of shout-outs per week (goal: 5+ per week)
- [ ] Track manager participation (goal: 100% of managers post at least 1 shout-out/month)
- [ ] Celebrate first service anniversary

**Month 6: Measure**
- [ ] Survey: "I feel recognized when I do good work" (goal: 70%+ favorable)
- [ ] Analyze: Are we hitting recognition goals?
- [ ] Iterate: What's working? What's not?

**Example:**

- Managers write handwritten notes to team members for great work

- Low cost
- High emotional impact (handwritten notes are rare and memorable)

## Implementation Checklist (Universal)

**Regardless of company size, follow this checklist:**

---

**Step 1: Define "Why"**
- [ ] What behaviors do we want to reinforce?
- [ ] What culture do we want to build?
- [ ] What problem are we solving? (low engagement? high turnover?)

---

**Step 2: Assess Current State**
- [ ] Survey: "I feel recognized when I do good work" (baseline score)
- [ ] How often is recognition happening today? (rarely? never?)
- [ ] What programs exist? (none? outdated "Employee of the Month"?)

---

**Step 3: Design Program**
- [ ] Choose components (peer recognition, spot bonuses, anniversaries, awards)
- [ ] Set budget (1-2% of payroll)
- [ ] Define rewards (what will we give? cash? experiences? time off?)

---

**Step 4: Get Buy-In**
- [ ] Present business case to leadership (engagement ‚Üí retention ‚Üí cost savings)
- [ ] Get budget approval
- [ ] Secure CEO sponsorship (must model recognition)

---

**Step 5: Build**
- [ ] Select platform (if using software)
- [ ] Create program guidelines (what gets recognized? how much?)
- [ ] Design communication plan (how will we launch?)

---

**Step 6: Train**
- [ ] Train managers on recognition (1-hour session)
- [ ] Provide templates and examples
- [ ] Set expectations (recognize at least 1 person/week)

---

**Step 7: Launch**
- [ ] CEO announces program (all-hands + email)
- [ ] Managers start recognizing
- [ ] Employees start using peer recognition tools

---

**Step 8: Measure**
- [ ] Track metrics (# recognitions/week, manager participation)
- [ ] Survey: "I feel recognized" (measure improvement)
- [ ] Analyze turnover (recognized vs. non-recognized employees)
- [ ] Calculate ROI

---

**Step 9: Iterate**
- [ ] What's working? (do more of it)
- [ ] What's not working? (fix or kill it)
- [ ] Gather feedback (quarterly pulse survey)
- [ ] Adjust budget/program as needed

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start small** - Don't need huge budget to build recognition culture (start with Slack channel)
2. **Make it frequent** - Recognition should happen weekly, not annually
3. **Empower managers** - Give managers budget and trust them to use it
4. **Measure impact** - Track sentiment, turnover, ROI
5. **Iterate** - Programs evolve as company grows

**Great recognition programs = frequent, meaningful, easy to use.**

## Overview: What is Peer Recognition?

**Peer recognition = Employees recognizing each other (not just top-down from managers)**

**Why it matters:**
- **Peers see 80% of the great work** that managers miss (cross-functional collaboration, helping teammates, quality of work)
- **Builds team bond** - Employees feel valued by colleagues, not just bosses
- **Scales recognition** - Can't rely on managers alone in a 500-person company
- **Reinforces culture** - When employees recognize each other for living values, culture becomes self-reinforcing

---

**Contrast:**

**Top-down only (traditional):**
- Manager recognizes Sarah ‚Üí Sarah feels valued by manager
- 99% of Sarah's teammates don't notice

**Peer-to-peer:**
- Tom (peer) recognizes Sarah publicly ‚Üí Sarah feels valued by colleague + entire team sees it ‚Üí Creates positive ripple effect

---

## Why Peer Recognition Fails (and How to Fix It)

### Failure Mode 1: No system (it just doesn't happen)

**What it looks like:**
- "We encourage peer recognition!" (but no tools or structure)
- Employees think about recognizing colleagues but never do it

**Why it fails:** Too much friction (how do I recognize someone? where do I do it?)

**Fix:** Make it ridiculously easy
- ‚úÖ Create #shoutouts Slack channel (1-click to post)
- ‚úÖ Use recognition software (Bonusly, Kazoo) - 1-click to give points
- ‚úÖ Add "recognition" to team meeting agendas (structured time)

---

### Failure Mode 2: Only power users participate

**What it looks like:**
- 5 employees post 90% of recognition
- Everyone else is silent

**Why it fails:** Cultural norm hasn't set in (most people don't see peers doing it, so they don't do it)

**Fix:** Make it visible and normal
- ‚úÖ Leadership models it (CEO, execs post shout-outs weekly)
- ‚úÖ Celebrate top recognizers (leaderboard, monthly shout-out)
- ‚úÖ Managers encourage participation in 1:1s ("Have you recognized anyone this week?")

---

### Failure Mode 3: Generic, low-quality recognition

**What it looks like:**
- "Great job @Tom!" (no specifics)
- "Thanks @Sarah for being awesome!" (vague)

**Why it fails:** Feels like checkbox behavior, not sincere

**Fix:** Train employees on great recognition
- ‚úÖ Provide examples (specific, values-aligned)
- ‚úÖ Encourage longer messages (not just 5 words)
- ‚úÖ Celebrate high-quality examples

---

## The 3 Models of Peer Recognition

### Model 1: Public Praise Only (Free, No Points)

**How it works:**
- Create dedicated channel (#shoutouts, #kudos, #wins)
- Employees post recognition publicly
- No points, no rewards - just appreciation

**Example platforms:**
- Slack (#shoutouts channel)
- Teams (Praise feature)
- Workplace by Meta
- Dedicated kudos board (physical or digital)

**Pros:**
- **Free** (no software cost)
- **Simple** (just post in Slack)
- **Builds culture** (public praise is powerful)

**Cons:**
- **No tangible reward** (only intrinsic motivation)
- **Can feel forgotten** (shout-outs scroll away in Slack)

**Best for:** Startups, budget-constrained teams, culture-first companies

---

### Model 2: Points-Based System (Peer-to-Peer Currency)

**How it works:**
- Every employee gets X points per month (e.g., 100 points)
- Employees give points to colleagues with a recognition message
- Points = $1 each, redeemable for rewards (gift cards, donations, experiences)
- Public feed of all recognition

**Example platforms:**
- **Bonusly** (most popular)
- **Kazoo**
- **Achievers**
- **Nectar**

**Example workflow:**
1. Tom gives Sarah 20 points for "Helping debug production issue üôå"
2. Sarah receives points + public recognition
3. Sarah accumulates points ‚Üí redeems for $120 Amazon gift card

**Pros:**
- **Tangible reward** (points = money)
- **Frequent** (employees can give points anytime)
- **Gamified** (leaderboards, badges, streaks)
- **Tracked** (analytics on who's recognizing whom)

**Cons:**
- **Cost** (platform fee + points budget)
- **Can feel transactional** (if not balanced with sincere messages)

**Best for:** Mid-market and enterprise companies with budget for software

---

### Model 3: Hybrid (Public Praise + Occasional Rewards)

**How it works:**
- Public Slack channel for recognition (free, frequent)
- Managers can "amplify" great peer recognition with a spot bonus

**Example:**
1. Tom posts in #shoutouts: "Sarah stayed late to help with the launch üôå"
2. Manager sees it ‚Üí Gives Sarah $100 spot bonus (amplifies peer recognition)

**Pros:**
- **Free daily recognition** (Slack channel)
- **Meaningful rewards for exceptional work** (manager spot bonuses)
- **Combines intrinsic + extrinsic motivation**

**Cons:**
- **Requires manager involvement** (not fully peer-driven)

**Best for:** Companies wanting frequent recognition without full points system

## Best Practices: Writing Great Peer Recognition

### Formula: Situation + Behavior + Impact

**Template:**
```
[Situation] When [specific situation happened],
[Behavior] you [specific thing they did],
[Impact] which [impact it had].

Thank you!
```

**Example 1: Helping a teammate**

‚ùå Bad: "Thanks @Sarah for helping!"

‚úÖ Good:
```
When I was stuck on the API integration yesterday, you jumped in to pair-program with me for 2 hours. You helped me find the auth bug I'd been chasing for days. That saved our launch timeline. Thank you! üôå
```

**Why it's good:**
- Specific situation (API integration)
- Specific behavior (pair-programmed for 2 hours, found bug)
- Clear impact (saved launch timeline)

**Example 2: Living a company value**

**Company value:** "Customer obsession"

‚ùå Bad: "Great customer service @Tom!"

‚úÖ Good:
```
Tom, when the customer called with an urgent bug on Friday at 5pm, you stayed on the call for 90 minutes walking them through the fix step-by-step. ```
```

**Why it's good:**
- Specific situation (Friday 5pm customer call)
- Specific behavior (stayed 90 min, walked through fix)
- Clear impact (customer renewing) + tied to company value (customer obsession)

---

**Example 3: Going above and beyond**

‚ùå Bad: "Nice job on the presentation @Linda!"

‚úÖ Good:

**Why it's good:**

## Measuring Peer Recognition Success

### Metric 1: Participation Rate

**Formula:**
```
Participation rate = (# employees who gave recognition this month / total employees) √ó 100%
```

**Example:**
- 200 employees
- 150 gave recognition in February
- Participation = 150 / 200 = 75%

**Benchmark:**
- Excellent: 80%+ (most employees participate)
- Good: 60-80%
- Needs improvement: 40-60%
- Poor: <40% (program is failing)

---

### Metric 2: Recognition Frequency

**Formula:**
```
Recognition frequency = # recognitions per month / # employees
```

**Example:**
- 200 employees
- 400 recognitions in February
- Frequency = 400 / 200 = 2.0 recognitions per employee

**Benchmark:**
- Excellent: 3+ recognitions per employee per month
- Good: 2-3
- Needs improvement: 1-2
- Poor: <1

---

### Metric 3: Manager Participation

**Formula:**
```
Manager participation = (# managers who gave recognition / total managers) √ó 100%
```

**Goal:** 100% of managers recognize at least 1 person per month

**If <100%:**
- Coach managers who aren't participating
- Make it part of manager performance expectations

---

### Metric 4: Sentiment

**Survey question:** "I feel recognized by my peers when I do good work"
- Strongly Disagree ‚Üí Strongly Agree (5-point scale)

**Track over time:**
```
Baseline (no program): 30% favorable
After 3 months: 55% favorable (+25 points)
After 6 months: 68% favorable (+38 points)
After 12 months: 75% favorable (+45 points)
```

**Goal:** 75%+ favorable

---

### Metric 5: Retention Impact

**Compare turnover:**

```
Employees who received peer recognition in past 12 months:
- Turnover: 7%

Employees who received NO peer recognition:
- Turnover: 19%

Peer recognition reduces turnover by 12 percentage points
```

**Financial impact:**
```
50 employees not recognized
19% turnover = 9.5 employees quit (round to 10)
Cost to replace: 10 √ó $30K = $300K

If peer recognition reduces turnover to 7%:
7% turnover = 3.5 employees quit (round to 4)
Cost to replace: 4 √ó $30K = $120K

Savings: $180K
```

**ROI:**
```
Peer recognition cost: $36K/year (Bonusly for 200 employees)
Savings from reduced turnover: $180K/year (partial company)

ROI = ($180K - $36K) / $36K √ó 100% = 400%

Every $1 spent on peer recognition saves $5 in turnover costs
```

## Troubleshooting: Low Adoption

### Problem 1: Only 20% of employees participating

**Root causes:**
- Employees don't know about the program
- Employees don't understand how to use it
- No cultural norm (people don't see others doing it)

**Fixes:**
- **Re-launch:** CEO announcement, all-hands demo, manager push
- **Gamify:** Run monthly challenge ("Most recognitions wins lunch with CEO")
- **Leadership models:** Execs recognize people publicly every week
- **Manager accountability:** Make it part of manager 1:1s ("Have you recognized anyone this week?")

---

### Problem 2: Recognition quality is low (generic "great job" messages)

**Root cause:** Employees don't know how to write great recognition

**Fixes:**
- **Train:** Share examples of great recognition (specific, values-aligned)
- **Feature:** Highlight "recognition of the month" at all-hands
- **Template:** Provide fill-in-the-blank template (Situation + Behavior + Impact)

---

### Problem 3: Same people get recognized repeatedly

**Root cause:** Visibility bias (people recognize colleagues they work with directly)

**Fixes:**
- **Encourage cross-functional recognition:** "This month, recognize someone outside your team"
- **Spotlight unsung heroes:** Managers nominate people who are doing great work but not getting recognized
- **Anonymized leaderboards:** Show departments with low recognition, encourage those teams

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Peer recognition scales culture** - Can't rely on managers alone
2. **Make it easy** - 1-click recognition (Slack, Bonusly, etc.)
3. **Leadership models it** - CEO and execs must participate
4. **Train on quality** - Specific, sincere, values-aligned recognition
5. **Measure and iterate** - Track participation, sentiment, turnover impact

**Great peer recognition = frequent, meaningful, embedded in daily work.**

# Values Awards Framework

Complete guide to designing annual awards programs that reinforce company values, including nomination processes, selection criteria, and award ceremonies.

---

## Overview: Values-Based Awards

**What are values awards?**

Annual recognition program where employees who exemplify company values win awards.

**Why values awards?**

1. **Reinforce culture:** Shows what living the values actually looks like
2. **Make values concrete:** Values posters on wall = abstract. Award winners = real examples
3. **Inspire others:** "If I want to win, I should act like Sarah acted"
4. **Retain top performers:** Awards recognize and reward best employees

**The shift:**

**Old model (‚ùå):**
- "Employee of the Year" (1 winner out of 500 employees)
- Chosen by execs behind closed doors
- Criteria vague ("who worked hardest?")

**New model (‚úÖ):**
- Multiple awards (1 per company value)
- Peer-nominated (anyone can nominate)
- Criteria clear (exemplifies specific value)
- Transparent process

---

## Step 1: Define Your Awards

### Map Awards to Company Values

**Formula:** 1 award per core company value

**Example: Company with 4 values**

| Company Value | Award Name | Description |
|---------------|------------|-------------|
| **Customer Obsession** | Customer Hero Award | For someone who went above and beyond for a customer |
| **Innovation** | Innovation Award | For someone who shipped a creative solution to a hard problem |
| **Ownership** | Ownership Award | For someone who owned a problem and drove it to completion |
| **Collaboration** | Team Player Award | For someone who helped others succeed |

**Result:** 4 awards (1 per value)

---

### Additional Award Categories (Optional)

**Beyond values, consider:**

**Rising Star Award**
- For high-potential individual contributor (not manager)
- Someone who's grown significantly this year
- Future leader

**Leadership Award**
- For best people manager
- Someone who develops, coaches, and inspires their team

**Lifetime Achievement Award**
- For long-tenured employee (10+ years)
- Recognizes career-long impact

**Founder's Award (CEO's Choice)**
- CEO selects one person who had outsized impact
- Can overlap with values awards or be separate

---

### Example: Full Awards Program

**Company:** 300 employees, 4 core values

**Awards:**

| Award | Description | Winner(s) |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Customer Hero** | Customer obsession | 1 winner |
| **Innovation** | Creative problem-solving | 1 winner |

## Step 2: Design the Nomination Process

### Open Nominations (Anyone Can Nominate)

**Why open nominations?**
- **Democratic:** Everyone has a voice
- **Uncovers hidden heroes:** Peers see work that managers miss
- **Drives engagement:** Employees feel involved in process

**Nomination form template:**

```
VALUES AWARD NOMINATION FORM

Nominee Name: ___________________________

Award Category: [Dropdown: Customer Hero, Innovation, Ownership, Team Player, Rising Star, Leadership]

Your Name (Nominator): ___________________________

1. What did this person do that exemplifies this award?
   (Specific example, 200-500 words)

   ___________________________________________
   ___________________________________________

2. What was the impact of their actions?
   (Business impact, customer impact, team impact)

   ___________________________________________
   ___________________________________________

3. How does this person embody [Company Value]?
   (Connect to specific value)

   ___________________________________________
   ___________________________________________
```

**Best practices:**

- **Keep it short:** 3 questions, max 500 words
- **Require specifics:** "What did they do?" (not just "they're great")
- **Deadline:** 2-3 weeks for nominations (enough time, not too long)

---

### Nomination Timeline

**Example: Annual awards announced in December**

| Date | Activity |
|------|----------|
| **Oct 1** | Open nominations (launch email, all-hands announcement) |
| **Oct 1-21** | Nomination period (3 weeks) |
| **Oct 22-31** | Selection committee reviews nominations |
| **Nov 1-15** | Committee selects finalists, conducts interviews (if needed) |
| **Nov 16-30** | Committee selects winners |
| **Dec 1-10** | CEO notifies winners privately, plans ceremony |
| **Dec 15** | Award ceremony (annual event or all-hands) |

**Total timeline:** 2.5 months from nomination launch to ceremony

---

### Encouraging Nominations

**Challenge:** How do you get employees to nominate people?

**Tactics:**

**1. Manager push**
- Managers encourage team to nominate in team meetings
- "Think about who on your team or cross-functionally did something amazing this year"

**2. Reminders**
- Email reminders at 1 week left, 3 days left, last day
- Slack posts in #general

**3. Make it visible**
- Share nomination count publicly ("50 nominations so far! Keep them coming!")

**4. Gamify (optional)**
- Team with most nominations gets lunch with CEO

**Goal:** 50-100+ nominations (for 300-employee company)

## Step 3: Selection Process

### Form Selection Committee

**Who should be on the committee?**

**Option 1: Cross-functional leadership**
- VP of each department (Eng, Sales, Marketing, Ops, People)
- CEO
- **Total:** 5-7 people

**Option 2: Peer committee**
- Mix of managers and individual contributors
- Rotates annually (different committee each year)
- Nominated by employees or selected by CEO
- **Total:** 5-10 people

**Best practice:** Include at least 1-2 individual contributors (not just execs)

---

### Selection Criteria

**How to evaluate nominations:**

**Scoring rubric (1-5 scale):**

| Criteria | Weight | Score (1-5) |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Exemplifies value** (Is this a clear example of the value?) | 40% | ___ |
| **Impact** (Did their actions have meaningful business/customer impact?) | 30% | ___ |
| **Specificity** (Is nomination specific with concrete examples?) | 20% | ___ |
| **Going above and beyond** (Is this exceptional, not just doing their job?) | 10% | ___ |

**Total score:** Out of 5.0

**Example:**

**Nomination:** Sarah for Customer Hero Award

**Evaluation:**

- **Exemplifies value:** 5/5 (stayed on call 2 hours to fix customer issue on Friday night - clear customer obsession)
- **Impact:** 5/5 (customer was going to churn, this saved the account worth $100K ARR)
- **Specificity:** 5/5 (nomination has concrete details, timestamps, customer quotes)
- **Above and beyond:** 5/5 (Friday night, not expected, went far beyond job duty)

**Total score:** 5.0 / 5.0 (top candidate)

---

### Selection Meeting

**Process:**

**Step 1: Committee members review all nominations (async)**
- Each committee member scores all nominations independently
- **Deadline:** 1 week before selection meeting

**Step 2: Selection meeting (2-3 hours)**
- Review top-scored nominations for each award
- Discuss: Do we agree? Any concerns?
- Vote (if needed): Each committee member votes for top 3 candidates
- **Output:** 1 winner per award category (or 2-3 finalists if tie)

**Step 3: Finalist interviews (optional)**
- If tie or need more context, interview finalists
- Ask: "Tell us about [the situation in your nomination]"
- Committee decides after interviews

**Step 4: Final decision**
- CEO has final say (or committee votes)
- Winners selected

---

### Handling Ties

**If 2 candidates are equally strong:**

**Option 1: Co-winners**
- Both win the award (split prize if monetary)
- Announce both at ceremony

**Option 2: Create "Honorable Mention"**
- 1 winner, 1-2 honorable mentions
- Honorable mentions get smaller prize + recognition

**Option 3: CEO tie-breaker**
- CEO reviews both nominations and picks one

## Step 5: Award Ceremony

### When to Announce Winners

**Option 1: Annual company event**
- Holiday party, summit, off-site
- Formal ceremony with speeches, videos
- **Pros:** Memorable, big celebration
- **Cons:** Requires event planning

**Option 2: All-hands meeting**
- December all-hands (or Q4 all-hands)
- CEO announces winners, plays video tributes
- **Pros:** Easier to execute, entire company attends
- **Cons:** Less "special" than dedicated event

**Option 3: Hybrid**
- Announce finalists at all-hands
- Award ceremony at holiday party

---

### Ceremony Format

**Recommended structure (60-90 minutes):**

**1. Opening remarks (CEO) - 5 min**
- "Tonight we celebrate our values. These awards recognize employees who embody what we stand for."
- Intro the process (# of nominations, selection committee)

**2. Award presentations - 8-10 min per award**
- Play video tribute (2-3 min)
  - Nomination highlights
  - Teammate testimonials
  - Photos/clips of winner
- CEO or exec announces winner
- Winner comes on stage, receives trophy
- Winner gives brief acceptance speech (2 min)

**3. Group photo - 5 min**
- All winners on stage together
- Company photographer captures moment

**4. Celebration - Remaining time**
- Dinner, drinks, dancing (if event)
- Mingle and congratulate winners

---

### Video Tribute Template

**For each winner, create 2-3 minute video:**

**Structure:**

**Part 1: Setup (30 sec)**
- Text on screen: "[Award Name]: [Winner Name]"
- Voiceover: "This year's [Award Name] goes to someone who..."

**Part 2: The Story (90 sec)**
- Show nomination highlights (what they did)
- Include photos/clips of winner in action
- Voiceover reads nomination excerpts

**Part 3: Testimonials (60 sec)**
- 3-4 teammates record short messages
- "Working with Sarah is... she always... I'm so grateful..."

**Part 4: Reveal (10 sec)**
- Text on screen: "Congratulations, Sarah!"
- Winner's photo

**Tools:**
- **DIY:** iMovie, Canva Video
- **Hire:** Video production company ($500-$2K per video)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Opaque process

**What it looks like:** Execs pick winners behind closed doors, no one knows how decisions are made

**Why it fails:** Employees don't trust process, feels political

**Fix:** Open nominations, clear criteria, committee with diverse perspectives

---

### Mistake 2: Same people win every year

**What it looks like:** Sarah wins every award, every year

**Why it fails:** Everyone else stops trying

**Fix:** Rule: Can't win same award 2 years in a row (can win different award)

---

### Mistake 3: Too many awards

**What it looks like:** 25 award categories, 50% of company wins something

**Why it fails:** Awards lose meaning (everyone wins = no one wins)

**Fix:** Keep it selective (5-10 awards max, <5% of company wins)

---

### Mistake 4: Vague nominations

**What it looks like:** Nomination says "Sarah is great!" (no specifics)

**Why it fails:** Committee can't evaluate

**Fix:** Require specific examples in nomination form (200+ words)

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Tie awards to values** - 1 award per company value
2. **Open nominations** - Anyone can nominate (democratic, uncovers heroes)
3. **Clear criteria** - Rubric for evaluating nominations (specific, impact, exemplifies value)
4. **Meaningful prizes** - $5K+ cash bonus + trophy + recognition
5. **Celebrate publicly** - Ceremony with video tributes, speeches, photos
6. **Measure impact** - Track participation, sentiment, retention

**Great values awards = reinforce culture, recognize top performers, inspire everyone.**
