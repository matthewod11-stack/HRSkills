# Exit Scenarios

Scenario-specific guidance for different types of departures.

---

## Scenario 1: Voluntary Resignation - Good Terms

**Typical reasons:** Better opportunity, career growth, relocation, personal reasons

### Characteristics
- Employee gives proper notice (2+ weeks)
- Good relationship with manager and team
- Performance is good or excellent
- Leaving for positive reasons

### Approach
**Tone:** Warm, appreciative, supportive, open door

**Manager's response:**
```
"I'm sad to see you go, but I'm excited for this opportunity for you. Thank you for
letting me know with proper notice - that's very professional and I appreciate it.

Let's create a transition plan together to set your replacement up for success. I'm
happy to be a reference and write you a LinkedIn recommendation.

You've made great contributions here, and I hope we can stay in touch."
```

### Checklist
- [ ] Accept resignation gracefully (don't guilt trip)
- [ ] Create comprehensive transition plan
- [ ] Conduct thorough exit interview
- [ ] Offer to be a reference
- [ ] Write LinkedIn recommendation
- [ ] Invite to alumni network
- [ ] Stay connected personally
- [ ] Make last day positive (team celebration)

### Don'ts
- ❌ Counteroffer (usually backfires)
- ❌ Take it personally or get emotional
- ❌ Guilt trip ("After all we've done for you...")
- ❌ Rush the exit (use full notice period)

### Alumni Strategy
- Strong candidate for boomerang (may return in 2-3 years)
- Will refer great candidates
- Can become customer or partner
- Positive brand ambassador

## Scenario 2: Voluntary Resignation - Poor Performance

**Typical reasons:** Writing on the wall, not meeting expectations, PIP avoidance

### Characteristics
- Performance has been discussed
- May have been on PIP or informal coaching
- Voluntary resignation to save face
- May be mutual understanding

### Approach
**Tone:** Professional, neutral, factual

**Manager's response:**
```
"Thank you for letting me know. I understand this is the right decision for both of
us. Let's create a brief transition plan and make this as smooth as possible."
```

### Checklist
- [ ] Accept resignation without debate
- [ ] Keep transition plan brief (1-2 weeks max)
- [ ] Conduct short, factual exit interview
- [ ] Don't make reference promises
- [ ] Revoke access promptly (but not before last day)
- [ ] Monitor knowledge transfer closely
- [ ] Keep announcement professional

### Don'ts
- ❌ Say "good riddance" (unprofessional)
- ❌ Skip exit interview (still valuable data)
- ❌ Burn bridges (maintain professionalism)
- ❌ Share performance details with team

### Alumni Strategy
- Low priority for alumni network
- Professional but not warm

## Scenario 3: Termination - Performance

**Reasons:** Not meeting expectations, repeated performance issues, PIP failure

### Characteristics
- Performance documented over time
- Previous conversations and warnings
- PIP completed without improvement
- Clear paper trail

### Approach
**Tone:** Direct, respectful, firm, final

**Termination Meeting Script:**
```
"[Name], as you know, we've discussed your performance over the past [X months],
including your Performance Improvement Plan. Unfortunately, we haven't seen the
improvement needed, so we've made the decision to end your employment effective today.

This is final. [HR] will walk you through the next steps, including your final
paycheck, COBRA, and returning company property. Do you have any questions?"
```

### Pre-Termination Checklist
- [ ] Consult HR and Legal
- [ ] Review all documentation (PIPs, warnings, etc.)
- [ ] Prepare severance package (if applicable)
- [ ] Plan termination meeting (private room, witness)
- [ ] Coordinate IT for access revocation (same day)
- [ ] Prepare final paycheck details
- [ ] Have boxes for personal belongings

### Termination Day Checklist
- [ ] Termination meeting (manager + HR, 10-15 min)
- [ ] Immediate access revocation
- [ ] Escort to collect belongings (if necessary)
- [ ] Provide written termination letter
- [ ] Provide severance details (if applicable)
- [ ] Collect company property
- [ ] Skip exit interview (or very brief)

### Post-Termination
- [ ] Announce to team (factual, brief)
- [ ] Redistribute responsibilities
- [ ] Monitor for security risks
- [ ] File documentation

### Don'ts
- ❌ Surprise termination (should follow documented performance issues)
- ❌ Argue or debate (decision is final)
- ❌ Emotional language (stay factual)
- ❌ Allow extended access (security risk)

### Legal Considerations
- **Document everything:** Performance conversations, warnings, PIP, improvement expectations
- **Consistency:** Treat similar situations similarly (avoid discrimination claims)
- **Witness:** Always have HR or another manager present
- **Review state laws:** Some states require immediate final paycheck
- **Consult lawyer:** Especially for protected classes or potential legal risk

**Announcement to team:**
```
"I wanted to let you know that [Name] is no longer with the company as of today.
We've made the decision to part ways. [Other person] will be taking over [their
responsibilities] going forward. If you have questions, please reach out to me
directly."
```

## Scenario 4: Termination - Misconduct

**Reasons:** Policy violation, harassment, theft, violence, insubordination

### Characteristics
- Serious policy violation
- May require investigation
- Immediate termination often necessary
- Legal risk

### Approach
**Tone:** Firm, immediate, by-the-book

**Termination Meeting:**
```
"[Name], we've completed our investigation into [incident]. Based on our findings,
we are terminating your employment effective immediately for violation of [policy].

This decision is final. Please collect your personal belongings now, and [HR] will
escort you out."
```

### Immediate Actions
- [ ] Suspend during investigation (with pay, to protect legal position)
- [ ] Conduct thorough investigation (interview witnesses, gather evidence)
- [ ] Consult Legal
- [ ] Termination meeting (keep it short, 5-10 min)
- [ ] Immediate access revocation (before meeting if security risk)
- [ ] Escort out (security or HR)
- [ ] No severance (typically)

### Don'ts
- ❌ Skip investigation (due process matters legally)
- ❌ Allow unsupervised access after termination
- ❌ Discuss details with team (privacy and legal risk)

### Legal Considerations
- **Investigate first:** Don't terminate based on rumors
- **Document everything:** Witness statements, evidence, timeline
- **Consistent enforcement:** Apply policies equally
- **Legal review:** Especially for harassment, discrimination, whistleblower claims
- **No defamation:** Don't share details outside need-to-know

**Announcement:**
```
"[Name] is no longer with the company as of today. I can't share details due to
privacy, but please reach out if you have questions about work coverage."
```

## Scenario 5: Layoff / Reduction in Force (RIF)

**Reasons:** Budget cuts, company pivot, downturn, restructure

### Characteristics
- Not performance-based
- Business decision
- May affect multiple people
- Severance typically provided

### Approach
**Tone:** Compassionate, honest, supportive, helpful

**Layoff Notification:**
```
"[Name], I have difficult news. Due to [business reason], we're reducing our team
size and your position is being eliminated. This is not a reflection of your
performance - it's a business decision.

Your last day is [date], and we're providing [X weeks] severance. We'll also offer
outplacement services to help you find your next role. I'm happy to be a reference
and make introductions to my network."
```

### Pre-Layoff Checklist
- [ ] Legal review (WARN Act if 50+ employees)
- [ ] Selection criteria (objective, documented)
- [ ] Severance packages prepared
- [ ] Outplacement services arranged
- [ ] Communication plan (who, when, how)
- [ ] Leadership aligned on messaging

### Layoff Day Checklist
- [ ] Notifications (all at once if possible, morning)
- [ ] Provide severance details in writing
- [ ] Offer outplacement services
- [ ] Be available for questions
- [ ] Announce to remaining team (same day)

### Post-Layoff
- [ ] Write LinkedIn recommendations
- [ ] Make introductions to network
- [ ] Maintain relationships
- [ ] Rehire first if roles come back

### Don'ts
- ❌ Use vague language ("restructure", "right-sizing")
- ❌ Surprise people (give context if possible)
- ❌ Cheap out on severance (reputation matters)
- ❌ Ghost people after they leave

### Severance Guidelines
- Junior: 2-4 weeks
- Mid-level: 4-8 weeks
- Senior: 8-16 weeks
- Executives: 3-6 months
- Plus: continued health insurance, extended equity vesting

### Legal Considerations
- **WARN Act:** 60 days notice required for 50+ layoffs
- **Selection criteria:** Must be objective, not discriminatory
- **Severance agreement:** Get legal review (includes release of claims)
- **State laws:** Some states have stricter requirements

**Company Announcement:**
```
Team,

Today we made the difficult decision to reduce our team size by [X%]. This was
driven by [honest business reason]. [X] teammates are affected.

This is not a reflection of their performance. These are talented people who
contributed significantly. We're providing severance and outplacement support.

For those staying, here's what this means... [be transparent about future].

I know this is hard. Let's support each other through this transition.
```

### Survivor Care
- [ ] Acknowledge grief and uncertainty
- [ ] Be transparent about future
- [ ] Redistribute work fairly
- [ ] Avoid survivor's guilt ("lucky to have a job")
- [ ] Check in on morale

## Scenario 6: Retirement

**Typical reasons:** Age, financial security, life transition

### Characteristics
- Planned and celebratory
- Long tenure typically
- Institutional knowledge
- Positive departure

### Approach
**Tone:** Celebratory, grateful, honoring

**Manager's response:**
```
"Congratulations! This is a huge milestone. You've contributed so much over [X
years], and we want to celebrate you properly.

Let's create a transition plan to transfer your knowledge. And we'd love to host
a retirement party to honor your time here."
```

### Checklist
- [ ] Celebrate contributions (party, gift, etc.)
- [ ] Extended knowledge transfer (2-3 months if possible)
- [ ] Document institutional knowledge
- [ ] Consider consulting arrangement
- [ ] Stay in touch
- [ ] Invite to company events

### Knowledge Transfer Priority
- High - retirees have decades of knowledge
- Plan 1-3 months of documentation and training
- Consider consulting retainer (they can help after departure)

### Don'ts
- ❌ Rush them out (use their knowledge)
- ❌ Forget to celebrate (this is a milestone)
- ❌ Lose touch (maintain relationship)

### Alumni Strategy
- Lifetime alumni status
- Invite to company reunions
- Consulting opportunities
- Brand advocates

## Scenario 7: Mutual Separation

**Typical reasons:** Role mismatch, company direction change, interpersonal fit

### Characteristics
- Neither party fully satisfied
- Amicable acknowledgment it's not working
- Negotiated departure terms
- Face-saving for employee

### Approach
**Tone:** Professional, respectful, collaborative

**Conversation:**
```
"I think we both agree this isn't the right fit. What if we work together on a
transition plan that works for both of us? I'm thinking [X weeks] notice, I'll be
a reference, and we'll support your job search."
```

### Checklist
- [ ] Negotiate departure terms (notice period, severance)
- [ ] Agree on reference/story ("pursuing other opportunities")
- [ ] Create transition plan
- [ ] Support job search if possible
- [ ] Keep it professional and respectful

### Don'ts
- ❌ Burn bridges
- ❌ Be vague (be clear about terms)
- ❌ Surprise anyone (mutual means discussed)

## Handling Special Situations

### Remote Employee Exit
- **Ship equipment:** Prepaid label, 1 week deadline
- **Virtual goodbyes:** Team Zoom call
- **Exit interview:** Video call
- **Access revocation:** Same process, even more important

### C-Level Executive Exit
- **Board involved:** May require board approval
- **Communication plan:** Internal first, then external/press
- **Transition plan:** 30-90 days typical
- **Consultant arrangement:** Common for continuity

### Founder Departure
- **Emotional:** Letting go of their baby
- **Equity considerations:** Vesting, board seat
- **Communication:** Tell whole story honestly
- **Legacy:** Honor contributions

## Crisis Situations

### Employee Threatens Legal Action
- **Stop:** Immediately involve Legal
- **Document:** Everything from this point forward
- **No admission:** Of wrongdoing
- **Professional:** Maintain professionalism

### Employee Becomes Hostile
- **Safety first:** If threatening, call security/police
- **Witness:** Don't meet alone
- **Document:** Record hostile behavior
- **Expedite:** May need immediate termination with escort

### Employee Refuses to Leave
- **Clear communication:** "Your employment is terminated effective now"
- **Security:** Escort if necessary
- **Trespassing:** Call police if they refuse to leave property

---

## Key Principles Across All Scenarios

1. **Treat people with respect** - How you treat people on the way out defines your culture
2. **Be clear and direct** - No vague corporate speak
3. **Document everything** - Legal protection
4. **Maintain confidentiality** - Don't gossip
5. **Plan the transition** - Don't leave team scrambling
6. **Stay professional** - Even when it's personal
7. **Think long-term** - People remember how they're treated

Great offboarding preserves relationships, knowledge, and your employer brand - regardless of departure reason.
